Becky Blanton, Spoke at TED Global about homelessness, was homeless for 18 months.
Written 16 Jan 2011
  • Join the YMCA or Gold's gym. Shower anytime, smell good and sleep in your van in the ones that are open 24-hours.
  • Clean homeless people (clothes and shower) get fewer hassles.
  • Rent a locker at the YMCA to keep your stuff safe.
  • Free wifi at most coffee shops lets you surf for jobs or write while feeling almost human and invisible and part of housed society at same time.
  • Get a part-time job through temp agency and work so you have someplace to go during the day. Better, get a night-job and sleep unmolested in any parking lot (in your car) during the day.
  • Use the giant top loading washers when you can. They cost more, but also hold more clothes.
  • If you can avoid it, don't fall for the temptation to drink or drug your depression away.
  • Never argue with a cop.
Not all homeless people are addicts or alcoholics. 75% are working or underemployed homeless and can't afford housing. They need tips too.

Ryan McGrath, Web Developer, Designer, Author, Awesome.
Written 8 May 2011 Â· Featured in The Huffington Post
I was homeless for a year when I was younger. Many of the tricks on here work, but I'll note one or two more:

  1. For a place to sleep, 24 hour Kinkos rocks! This might sound odd to some, but it totally works wonders. If you happen to have a laptop it's much easier to pull off, but you basically go in, start working, and "fall asleep" on your laptop. Most of the time they won't question you, since a lot of college kids will work out of them and inevitably crash while working.  The trick here, though, is to be clean shaven and not threatening. If you smell like you just got done dumpster diving, you'll be picked up on in a heartbeat and asked to leave. Common sense works wonders when you're homeless.
  2. 24 hour "Walmart Garden" sections I'm not joking about this (sadly). Works best in the summer, but most Walmart garden sections are outside and easy to get into unnoticed. Find a decent low-traffic section, you can sleep between plants and such. The people who work here will almost never notice you, because to be honest, 90% of the time they don't want to be there anyway.  Yes, the last sentence sounds a bit cruel, but sometimes being realistic entails this.
  3. Libraries are your new best friends Most libraries these days have computers you can use for free, so use the time to submit job applications and do some basic networking. If you're on Facebook for any of this period... well, this kind of explains why you're homeless.  On top of that, libraries are just a huge source of knowledge. I probably learned more when I was homeless than any of my schooling years, as I had so much free time I'd just sit in libraries and Borders all day, reading anything I could get my hands on. If you play this right, it can easily act as somewhat of a "mock degree". If you're smart about this, you'll grab a ton of books and grab one of the "study rooms" that are usually littered throughout libraries.  It's also worth noting that this trick isn't new, though - it probably won't be very useful in inner city situations. In more rural areas it works very well, though - inner city libraries will make you feel less human due to the activities of other homeless people.
  4. Quit talking to other homeless people I'll get untold amounts of hell for this comment, but it was quite possibly the best thing I ever did for myself during my time like this. It's very easy to think that homeless people are just down on their luck and in an unfortunate spot, and while this can certainly be true for some, I found that the majority of homeless people are just abusing the system because they can. The more you hang around lazy people who game the system, the higher your chances of never getting out become.  If you have friends who aren't homeless, do everything in your power to keep hanging out with them for some sense of normalcy. Getting through this on your own (in a mental capacity, not talking handouts here) is incredibly difficult (but ultimately so worthwhile).
  5. Public pools = decent showers Now that I've probably grossed out everyone reading this, let's get to the point: if you go early, community pools are often free and a great way to wash up for the day and get some exercise in.  Besides, I guarantee you're actually not the worst thing to end up in that pool over the course of a day.

Most of these tricks rely on a very important concept, though: pay attention to your perception. It took me such a ridiculously long time to realize this (maybe due to my age at the time, I don't know), but the way the world perceives you plays such a heavy factor in what you get out of it. This might sound vain, but it's 100% accurate, and you're doing yourself a disservice if you try to pretend otherwise.

On one final note, don't eat Dollar Menu because it's the cheapest food available. It will bite you in the ass. :|

I've spent a large portion of my life back packing & camping. Some of the skills & experiences I've picked up could be helpful in a homeless situation.

****Note: I don't advocate breaking any laws. This is for information purposes only****

The first & most obvious problem to solve is Shelter. 

  1. Tents - Having a tent makes shelter much easier in a survival situation.  You can set them up in the woods, along the river bank, under bridges or in even in a park. 
  2. 24-Hour Stores - Many places are open 24 hours and can be a safe haven from the cold night.  You'll often see many homeless people sleeping during the day.  That's because they spent the night up & moving.  In the winter finding a place with heat is essential, especially in the north, where temperatures drop well below zero.  With a little a change you could set out the night in a diner, just make sure to buy a coffee.
  3. Bridges & Underpasses - These locations aren't ideal but offer some protection from the elements.  In the summer these concrete shelters retain a lot heat from the day & will help keep you warm for the night. In the winter they become a deathtrap because concrete is such heat-sink.
  4. Construction Sites - Many construction sites are located across the country.  They can work as a shelter in a pinch.  Note* most construction sights are clearly marked "no trespassing".  Do this at your own risk, & be sure to be out before sunrise because construction workers are early risers & tough as nails. 
  5. Abandoned houses - Across Detroit there are countless abandoned homes being reclaimed by the community. Some of these places are being claimed for penny's.  In a homeless/survival situation they could be a lifesaver.
  6. Dumpsters! - Other than a source of countless supplies they could also be used as a shelter.  Of course I mean the large recycling dumpsters.  Is it pouring rain? Are you desperate? Jump right in. 
  7. Parking Garage - Some parking garages are closed for the night.  These lots seldom have guards.  Here you can find shelter in an elevator or stairwell.  If you have canned heat or a small coleman stove you can even cook something on the roof.
  8. Airports, Bus, & Train stations - Not a long term solution, but works in a pinch.  You can spend sometime in at station before anyone will harass you.  Especially if you have a bag.  "Waiting on someone" can buy you plenty of time to take the load off.  Just don't go in filthy.
  9. Homeless Shelters - Not really hack... hmm
  10. Jail - Here we go.  If you're on the verge of death (cold, starvation) get arrested.  I suggest doing it for something petty. Don't go exposing your self.  Just go for the good ol' public intoxication. You'll at least get a place to sleep.  Also if you refuse to give your name you could buy some more time. Just pretend its civil disobedience.
  11. ER - If you have no other options, go to the emergency room.  Complain about something minor & you'll get bumped to the back of the list.
  12. Churches - Shelter can be found at various churches across the country. sometimes you'll even get a meal.
  13. Stay with someone - Stay with a friend or find someone to stay with. Use Page on craigslist.com  &   Stay With Locals & Make Travel Friends to find a place to crash.


Next would be Food

  1. Hunting - Whether using a rifle, pellet gun, or snare, theres plenty of food roaming the countryside. You just have to get it.
  2. Dumperster Diving!!! - Probably the best method of obtaining literally anything.  America is built on consumerism.  Any place that sales something perishable will have waste. Key places are Pizza places, bagel shops, pastry, bread shops, Krispy Kreme, etc.  Heres some Dumpster Diving resources: Dumpster Diving - the world-wide guide to dumpster diving   &  Dumpster Diving: Fun with Garbage â€¢ /r/DumpsterDiving
  3. Work - A lot of odd jobs can be found on craigslist & local bulletin boards.
     
  4. Soup Kitchen - Most major cities offer food twice a day.  Find when & where.


Transportation

  1. Train Hopping.  You can pretty much make it across the country by train hopping. Train hopping also offers a form a mobile shelter.  I highly recommend researching before attempting.  Here's some resources.  Train Hopping Guide -Freight Hopping  & Types of Train Cars to Ride
  2. Hitch Hiking - Self Explanatory


Warmth

  1. Wood Gas Stove - You'll need something to cook with and keep you warm.  A homemade stove made out of soup cans does the trick perfectly.     Wood Gasification Stove - 
  1. Overalls - Insulated overalls for working or hunting will go a long way in keeping you warm.
  2. Sleeping bag - A must have for long term exposure to the elements.
  3. Tarp - Keeping dry is a major factor in retaining warmth. Use as a makeshift roof, or simply wrap around a sleeping bag.
  4. Cardboard or Foam Pad - Some sort of padding to distance yourself from the cold ground, thats sole intent is sucking every ounce of warmth from your body.
  5. Waterproof bag or Trash Compactor bag - Something to keep your gear in.


Tech -  Nowadays Wifi hotspots are everywhere.  If you have a device that is capable then finding internet access shouldn't be an issue.  Keeping that device charged is an issue.  Library's are great but I would recommend a solar charger.  Fairly large charges can mounted onto a backpack and charge your device with a few hours.

My advice is neither pithy nor pretty but it is informed by my experience of just this. 2 sentences and then some clarifying remarks. 

Find support and find it quickly.
Do not be ashamed of your condition.

To hell with pride and any forces that keep you in your state.

Resources always exist. Sometimes they are easier to access then others.

Couch Surf, find a homeless shelter, apply for public benefits. Do whatever you can to move yourself out of your present situation and into stability again.

Seek to better yourself at every step. If the cause of your homelessness is purely fiscal seek financial counselling. If it is career related career counselling, if due to some natural disaster, seek emergency management aid. If substance or mental health related seek substance abuse counselling or mental health services.

For many shame is deeply encoded in the experience of becoming homeless. This emotion is entirely and completely counterproductive. Yes there may wall have been things you might have done better, and those things might have prevented your arrival at your current state. But those things did not come to pass. You arrived at your current state regardless. Now is the time to look up not look back.

If you're homeless in ENGLAND - obtain a set of fire brigade keys (FB keys). These can be bought on Amazon for under a tenner (possibly in hardware stores too). Each is designated a number, the most useful being FB 11 and FB 14. FB locks are used all over the country on changing rooms as well as a host of other council run buildings (where the heating is often left on). This will provide you somewhere to get out of the cold and get a good night's sleep.

The trick of course is to move around and not stay in one place for too long. As a council worker I often get complaints from members of the public about people sleeping rough in parks (complaints I do nothing about, I should add). The complaints  come from other park users but only after the homeless people have been there for several days - so it seems to me that moving around is one way of avoiding this.

For food - as far as I know the HARE KRISHNAS still give a good nourishing free meal to anyone that turns up at their door. So maybe finding where the local Hare Krishna temple is located is an idea.

Volunteer for research at some university. Sometimes, you can get 24-hour access to certain buildings, which means that you can sleep in their sofas.

Also, the most prolific mathematician of the 20th century (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pau...) was a hobo who lived in other the houses of other mathematicians. They even had to do his financial bills for him

But anyways, seriously, there are people who could use some free labor, and might provide you with some living arrangements if you can show that you're reliable and *not* a threat (and if you can show that you can help them with their research without wasting too much of their time) [1]. And professors are much more liberal than most other people anyways (it's easier to find their contact emails, and they don't mind it if you Google them), so they are who I'd approach (just first make sure that they aren't living with children). Or maybe grad students.

If you're university staff or have a 24-hour building permit of any kind, you can sleep in the university buildings all the time (now, some departments prohibit it, but over here, the undergraduate library and the physics building won't protest it)

Also, the physics building here has showers in the basement. If you're desperate, you can always use the safety showers anyways. ;)

If you're an interesting person, you can also try couchsurfing.com or something. There might be people who are liberal enough to take you in as long as you can prove that you aren't a threat.

There's also sleepinginairports.com (and yes, some people do manage to stay in them for weeks), but airports generally aren't known for being liberal

Mitchell Tsai, Upper-middle-class homeless for 7 years ("voluntary homeless")
Written Apr 30

Mostly for US:

Sleeping without a car:
  - In San Francisco, pay $10-15 for 2-3 hr bus ride to casino, where they give you $15-20 Free Play.  Cash Free Play using Video Blackjack or Video Poker.  One of my friends had all their stuff stolen, and slept on the bus rides.
  - [International also] In airports, bring rollaboard luggage, and locking cable to connect it to chairs...maybe even fake boarding pass.  SleepingInAirports is helpful.  I don't feel as safe sleeping in train & bus stations.

Sleeping in a car/van:
  - Pick a neighboorhood where your vehicle fits in.
  - Some areas like Los Angeles, sleeping is now legal 24 hrs/day
  - Other areas 3-5 am may be illegal.  Park in a business lot, set an alarm, and move onto the street before 8 am.
  - Most Wal-Marts are sleeper-friendly (some exceptions), helpful if they have 24 hr bathrooms
  - Truck stops, Interstate rest stops
  - DON'T use the same spot every day... Duh...

Showers:
   - I have $20/yr Fitness club membership (yes $20/yr...not $20/month). Look at coverage areas of your club for where you like to travel
  - Most backcountry/park areas have $3-5 showers (not always with hot water).  Sometimes these are free after certain hours (e.g. Yosemite Curry Village showers are free after 10 pm)
  - Cities are often more difficult for cheap showers (wish it weren't).  Showers for homeless may even be $10-15.
  - In cities, public pools (the shower, not the pool, ick!), college gyms, yoga studios (may have $5-8-10-12 cheap "community classes"), airports (some have free/pay showers), fitness gyms ($10-15-20 daily fee, or "free guest visit"), hotel pools or spas (daily fee $10-15-25), any building/company with exercise-related activities where showers might be available.
 - Sleep in hotel spas in the daytime, or in the pool area...after paying the day-use fee for the spa or pool.
  - Sometimes the bathrooms near the hotel pool (but not in the paid area) have a shower.  Bring your own soap.
  - Some bicycle-commuting facilities (Saw this recently, but never tried it yet.)
  - Truck stops have showers, but usually on the expensive-side ($8-12).

Illegal showers:
  - For people trying the illegal route (not recommended), can sometimes sneak into all of the above.
  - In Vegas, useful to get used hotel room keys for entry into pool areas (with showers).  Mandalay Bay, Bellagio, and LV Hilton (now closed) used to be the only places that swiped room keys.  In most other cities, cards must be swiped or placed in locks.  However, locks are not always functional... (and may be broken for years...)

Ben Mordecai, Living Intentionally
Written Apr 7, 2012
  • Every phone always has 911 service. Even cell phones without service can call 911. This is worth having.
  • Get a Gmail account and a Google voice account. This is where people can get in touch with you: family, work leads, etc. You can forward any calls to whatever prepaid cell phone you may have at the time but keep the same number.
  • When you are disposed to pan handle, try asking for specific things like a toothbrush, toothpaste, safety razor, a new shirt, and so on rather than money. Everyone is afraid you are going to spend whatever they give you on alcohol or drugs. By making specific requests you can find more helpful people some of whom are genuinely willing to give you more than the value of the quarters in their pocket.

However, there was a one year period when I was forced to be in an urban environment a couple of nights each week, far away from home.

A bike and a backpack were all I had. The bike was valuable, as it gave me a much wider range in the city to pick sleeping areas from.

The backpack held a change of clothes, a sleeping bag, and, the main "hack" of my answer â€“ a Hennessy sleeping hammock.

Note: this was not my setup, but a decent representation.

The Hennessy was a game changer. All I needed was a small patch of trees (or just two!) for a little cover, and I could go from riding to sleeping in less than 10 minutes.

Daniel Helman, Geoscientist - Nonprofit Executive Director - ESL Teacher - Sculptor w/ Moldm...
Written Jan 13, 2014
This applies to the "working poor" as well as those without.

Food banks are often very easy to sign up for and receive groceries.  There will usually be a centralized list of food banks, perhaps organized by the city government where you are.

In Long Beach, it is very easy to get food.  For many, the process is: (1) arrive at the location, (2) get a number, (3) wait, usually about an hour, (4) write your name, zip code or other information on a paper for accounting purposes, and (5) have your pick of some wonderful food.

The trade off is the time involved in waiting for your turn.  The food selection is very good, and one always feels a sense of camaraderie.  This is a good thing to try, if finances are tight!
